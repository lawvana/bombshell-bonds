What Is a Property Bond

Your equity.
Their freedom.

A property bond — also called a surety bond or real property bond — lets a property owner use their home or land equity as collateral to secure a defendant's release from jail, instead of paying a bail bondsman's non-refundable premium.

The court places a lien on the property. If the defendant appears at all court dates, the lien is released at the end of the case. You keep all your money.

Attorney Barker handles the entire process: title examination, petition drafting, court filing, and lien release — one flat fee, start to finish.

FAQ

Common questions.

How much equity do I need in my property?
NC courts generally require the property's equity — its market value minus all outstanding mortgages and liens — to be at least 150% of the bond amount. So for a $50,000 bond, you'd need at least $75,000 in clear equity. We verify this during the title examination, which we conduct as part of our service.
What happens to my property if the defendant skips court?
If the defendant fails to appear, the court can order forfeiture of the bond. This means the lien on your property could be enforced. This is why we counsel all property bond clients thoroughly before filing — you need to be confident the defendant will appear. We also track court dates and provide reminders as part of our service.
How long does the process take?
In most cases, we can complete the title examination, prepare the petition, and get to a bond hearing within 24–48 hours of your initial call. Emergency situations can sometimes be handled faster. The timeline depends on court scheduling and the complexity of the title work.
Can I use property I own jointly with someone else?
Yes, but all owners must consent and sign the bond documents. If the property is jointly owned, all co-owners become sureties on the bond. We explain the obligations fully before anyone signs.
Is a property bond available in federal court?
Yes. We handle property bonds in both the Eastern District of NC (EDNC) and the Middle District of NC (MDNC). Federal bonds have additional requirements — the court must approve the surety — and we've handled both districts extensively.
What does the flat fee cover?
The flat fee covers: property title examination, equity analysis, preparation of the bond petition and all supporting documents, filing with the court, attorney appearance at the bond hearing, and lien release coordination at the conclusion of the case.
